Preliminary Tests into the Reed–Solomon Proximity Gap Conjecture

This page is an attempt to give a concrete flavor of why the Reed–Solomon proximity gap question remains unresolved, and why it is not easy to settle even through direct computation. The issue is not merely that formal proof is hard. Rather, once one gets near the relevant boundary, the combinatorics themselves begin to work against naive intuition.

My interest in this problem came from a basic but important puzzle: if one wants to test whether a received word has only a small number of nearby Reed–Solomon codewords, then why not simply search for a counterexample computationally? In principle, a single counterexample would be enough to refute the wrong conjectural statement. In practice, however, the difficulty is subtler. Small finite cases can reveal the underlying obstruction, but they do not automatically settle an asymptotic conjecture, and exhaustive testing becomes expensive much sooner than one might expect.

The guiding idea throughout was to study the worst-case list size L_t = \max_r |\{c \in C : d(r,c) \le t\}|\) at and just beyond the Johnson radius, and to use small exhaustive experiments to see whether the difficulty already appears locally.

1. Why this question is hard in the first place

Reed–Solomon codes are among the best understood codes in mathematics, so it is tempting to think that their proximity behavior should also be well understood. But the relevant question is not just ordinary unique decoding. Instead, the issue is what happens when one moves beyond the classical unique-decoding radius and begins to ask whether the number of nearby low-degree explanations stays controlled or can grow abruptly.

This is where the difficulty enters. At small radius, one usually has uniqueness or near-uniqueness. Near the Johnson radius, the list size is often still modest. But one step beyond that threshold, many different low-degree polynomials can suddenly become compatible with the same received word. That is not simply a failure of computation. It reflects a real combinatorial crowding phenomenon.

A major lesson from the experiments is that the Johnson boundary is not just a decorative proof artifact. In several small prime-field examples, it behaves like a genuine transition point.

2. The computational philosophy used here

Rather than beginning with sophisticated asymptotic machinery, the approach here was deliberately concrete:

  • fix a small triple (q,n,k),
  • generate all message polynomials of degree less than k,
  • evaluate them to obtain the full Reed–Solomon codebook,
  • generate all received words in F_q^n,
  • and measure, for each radius t, the maximum number of nearby codewords.

This is intentionally brute-force. The point was not to optimize immediately, but to make the small cases fully transparent. If a local blow-up in list size appears even there, then the source of difficulty is already visible in the combinatorics, not only in the weakness of the machine.

Basic workflow idea
# Generate every polynomial of degree < k
# Evaluate at n points to obtain all codewords
# Enumerate all received words in F_q^n
# For each radius t:
#     compute the number of codewords within distance <= t
# Record the worst-case list size L_t

3. Why computational testing becomes hard surprisingly quickly

The brute-force plan sounds modest when written at a high level, but the growth is severe. There are q^k codewords and q^n received words. Even for small prime fields, that means the search space expands quickly. So there are really two layers of difficulty:

  • the mathematical difficulty of understanding when lists stay small, and
  • the computational difficulty of scanning enough received words to identify genuine worst cases.

This is why small examples are informative but not decisive. They can reveal the mechanism of failure, yet they do not automatically tell us what happens uniformly across broader parameter families.

4. The parameter meanings

Symbol Meaning
q Field size
n Block length
k Dimension, equivalently degree bound < k
d Minimum distance n-k+1
J Johnson radius, often written as n - sqrt(n(k-1))
L_t Worst-case list size within radius t

5. What the small experiments suggested

The most striking pattern in the uploaded draft was the repeated jump in worst-case list size when moving from the Johnson radius to one step beyond it. In several prime-field examples, the list size at the Johnson point was still small, but the list size at J+1 became dramatically larger. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Even more revealing, these jumps often matched simple interpolation counts. For instance, when k=2, agreement on two coordinates can determine a degree-1 polynomial, so one naturally sees counts like C(5,2)=10. When k=3, agreement on three coordinates can determine a quadratic, and one sees counts like C(6,3)=20. That is a strong clue that the obstruction is geometric, not accidental. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

Representative interpretation pattern
For k = 2 and n = 5:
  agreement on 2 coordinates can determine a degree-1 polynomial
  number of coordinate pairs = C(5,2) = 10

For k = 3 and n = 6:
  agreement on 3 coordinates can determine a degree-2 polynomial
  number of coordinate triples = C(6,3) = 20

So the emerging picture is this: once the allowed radius is large enough that many different k-point interpolation patterns become feasible, the list can grow for structural reasons. This helps explain why the question is hard. One is not simply fighting runtime; one is fighting real combinatorial multiplicity.

6. Why this still does not settle the conjecture

This is the subtle point. A local blow-up in finite examples can be highly informative and still fail to disprove the exact formal statement one cares about. Many conjectures of this kind are asymptotic, or involve particular ranges of rate, distance, or testing parameters. So a bad finite example may illuminate the mechanism without closing the question.

That is why these tests are best understood as narrowing the intuition gap rather than the theorem gap. They tell us why naive optimism is dangerous. They do not, by themselves, provide a full positive or negative resolution.

Honest summary
Small exhaustive tests can reveal the obstruction.
They do not automatically settle the asymptotic conjecture.
The wall is not only computational.
The wall is also combinatorial.

7. Why prime-field tests mattered

One lesson that emerged in the earlier exploration was that it is better to test genuine finite-field instances, especially over prime fields such as q=7 or q=13, rather than to rely too heavily on toy modular experiments. Prime-field cases carry more mathematical weight, and when the same qualitative jump appears there as well, it becomes much harder to dismiss the behavior as an artifact of an unnatural model.

This is precisely why the prime-field runs were especially interesting: they suggested that the beyond-Johnson crowding phenomenon is not a fluke of one small setup, but reflects something more persistent in the underlying interpolation geometry. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

8. What makes this question interesting to me

What drew me to this problem was the gap between the simplicity of the initial question and the difficulty of making real progress. At first glance, it seems that one should be able either to prove a good bound or search for a counterexample. But once one tries to test the conjecture computationally in a serious way, the problem becomes a meeting point of algebra, combinatorics, asymptotic interpretation, and brute-force feasibility.

In that sense, this project is not only about Reed–Solomon codes. It is also about how mathematical conjectures resist both proof and computation for different but related reasons.

9. Where I would take the experiments next

The most natural next steps are not simply “use a larger machine,” but to test more systematically in true prime fields while keeping the experimental questions focused. Some especially natural directions are:

  • track L_J and L_{J+1} over additional prime-field examples,
  • hold the rate approximately fixed while increasing n modestly,
  • check when the observed list size keeps matching interpolation-count heuristics,
  • and try to distinguish what is truly asymptotic from what is merely local but dramatic.

That would not settle the conjecture immediately, but it would sharpen the boundary between what seems computationally inaccessible and what seems combinatorially inevitable.
