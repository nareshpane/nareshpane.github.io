Testing the Limits of the nauty Package for Checking Ramsey Number (5,5)

This project grew out of a sustained attempt to understand how far one can push the nauty package, together with supporting scripts in Bash and Python, in computational experiments related to the Ramsey number R(5,5). The focus here is not merely on generating graphs, but on identifying where the real computational bottlenecks appear, which restrictions help, and which seemingly reasonable ideas fail in practice.

In my own work on this question, the challenge was not simply “run a graph generator and test everything.” The actual difficulty was much more structural: for graphs on 43 vertices, the search space becomes enormous, and even when one narrows the edge range and imposes degree restrictions, the number of candidates remains too large for naive brute force. This forced a shift from simple generation toward screening, pruning, sampling, non-isomorphic reduction, graph6 handling, and careful debugging of witness-testing code.

Official nauty site: https://pallini.di.uniroma1.it/

1. Why nauty is relevant here

The nauty package is one of the standard tools for generating and processing graphs up to isomorphism. For Ramsey-type computation, this matters because many labelled graphs are merely relabellings of the same underlying structure. If those duplicates are not removed, the search becomes even more infeasible than it already is.

In the R(5,5) setting, one is interested in two-colourings of the edges of a complete graph that avoid a monochromatic K5. Equivalently, one can represent one colour class as a graph G and the other as its complement. Then a colouring is a witness against R(5,5) ≤ n precisely when both G and its complement avoid K5.

That equivalence makes graph generation and graph testing natural. But it also reveals the real problem: the ambient space is so large that one cannot hope to enumerate all candidates at the relevant scale without very aggressive reduction.

2. What I was actually trying to do

The practical goal was to explore candidates around the 43-vertex regime, especially under constraints such as edge ranges and degree windows, and to check whether those candidates could serve as witnesses for the Ramsey problem. The broad plan involved:

  • generating graphs with geng, gengL, and genrang,
  • restricting edge counts and degree ranges where possible,
  • using pickg to post-filter by degree properties,
  • parsing graph6 output,
  • testing graphs and complements for the presence of a K5,
  • and trying to deduplicate non-isomorphic candidates before heavier checking.

This sounds straightforward in outline, but each step turned out to have technical complications.

3. First steps: how nauty is used at a basic level

At a beginner level, some of the most useful commands in nauty for this sort of work are geng, gengL, pickg, and listg.

Example: generate non-isomorphic graphs with small size constraints
./geng -q -d2 -D3 8 10

This generates non-isomorphic graphs on 8 vertices with exactly 10 edges, minimum degree 2, and maximum degree 3.

Example: a larger generation on 43 vertices with an exact edge count
./gengL -q -d0 -D5 43 10

The L version is designed to handle larger output streams more flexibly, though “larger” quickly becomes relative once one moves toward dense 43-vertex cases.

Example: generate then post-filter by degree window
./gengL -q -D10 43 10 | ./pickg -q -D6:10 > ~/Documents/graphs_43v_10e_maxdeg_6_to_10.g6

This kind of pipeline became important because some constraints are easier to impose directly during generation, while others are more naturally enforced afterward.

4. The main difficulty: the search space explodes

The hardest issue was not syntax. It was scale. Once the target moved toward graphs on 43 vertices with edge counts in a range that might plausibly matter for R(5,5), the number of candidates became overwhelming.

One representative command was:

Dense search attempt with degree restrictions
./gengL -q -d18 -D24 43 387:451

This is already highly restricted compared with the full space: it narrows the edge range and imposes a fairly tight degree window. Yet in practice, even that is still too large to treat as a routine laptop computation. This was one of the clearest lessons from the project: plausible-looking restrictions are often nowhere near enough.

In other words, there is a large gap between “I have reduced the search space mathematically” and “the remaining search is computationally manageable.”

5. Why edge and degree restrictions help, but not enough

It is natural to hope that if one restricts both the total number of edges and the degree range, one will eliminate most bad candidates from the start. That hope is partially justified. Degree windows do remove obvious extremes, and edge-range restrictions can focus attention on graphs that look more plausible as witnesses.

But the experiments made clear that these constraints do not magically compress the problem into something small. Even after discarding huge portions of the full graph space, what remains can still be astronomically large. This is why the project gradually moved away from a naive “generate all, then test all” mindset toward a more layered strategy.

6. A practical complication: genrang is useful, but limited

Because exhaustive generation became too expensive, another line of attack was to use random generation by edge count. A typical pattern was:

Random generation over a range of exact edge counts
for m in $(seq 387 451); do
    ./genrang -q -g -e"$m" 43 100000
done > raw_random_43v_387_451.g6

This is attractive because it lets one sample many candidates without insisting on complete enumeration. But it introduces a new problem: genrang does not naturally enforce all the structural restrictions one might want, especially tight degree windows of the kind that seemed relevant here.

So although random generation helped with memory and time pressure, it did not remove the need for downstream filtering. In practice, that meant shifting computational burden rather than eliminating it.

7. Post-filtering with pickg

Once direct generation under all desired constraints became difficult, the natural workaround was to generate first and filter afterward. Commands of the following form were central:

Filter by maximum degree window
./gengL -q -D8 43 44 | ./pickg -q -D8:8
Filter by maximum degree range
./gengL -q -D10 43 10 | ./pickg -q -D6:10

This is a sensible tactic, but it creates a trade-off. The less restrictive the upstream generator is, the more candidates flow into the filter. At that point, the pipeline may still be expensive simply because the intermediate stream is too large.

So one of the recurring themes in this project was that a pipeline can be logically correct and still be computationally poor.

8. Debugging the witness test: when “all graphs are witnesses” is suspicious

A particularly important difficulty arose after generating candidates and running Python checks for whether a graph and its complement both avoided a K5. At one point, the program appeared to classify all or nearly all generated graphs as witnesses. That looked wrong, and it was the kind of output that should trigger suspicion.

There are at least two reasons such a result can appear:

  • the testing code has a bug, especially in graph6 parsing or in the logic that checks the complement, or
  • the generated graphs are so sparse that they obviously cannot contain a K5, and their complements become the real issue.

This mattered because I was not working with toy examples only. I had to distinguish between a genuine mathematical phenomenon and a programming error. That forced careful re-examination of graph6 parsing, complement construction, and what the edge range itself implied.

Illustrative Python structure for a witness check
import networkx as nx
from itertools import combinations

def has_k5(G):
    nodes = list(G.nodes())
    for subset in combinations(nodes, 5):
        H = G.subgraph(subset)
        if H.number_of_edges() == 10:
            return True
    return False

def is_ramsey_55_witness(G):
    comp = nx.complement(G)
    return (not has_k5(G)) and (not has_k5(comp))

The logic is simple enough in principle, but in practice the real danger lies earlier: did the graph get parsed correctly, are the vertices correct, was the graph6 header handled properly, and did the complement get taken on the intended vertex set?

9. Graph6 handling was not a side issue

For this project, graph6 was not just a file format in the background. It was an operational bottleneck. Once outputs were being piped through different tools, saved to files, read back in Python, and re-tested, the exact encoding details mattered.

One practical point was that graph6 may include the optional header >>graph6<<. Another was that for small enough graphs, the first character encodes the order of the graph. If that is mishandled, every downstream conclusion becomes unreliable.

Example of the sort of graph6 issues that required attention
def strip_graph6_header(line):
    line = line.strip()
    if line.startswith(">>graph6<<"):
        line = line[len(">>graph6<<"):]
    return line

The broader lesson was that in combinatorial computation, apparently minor encoding details can completely invalidate large batches of results.

10. Non-isomorphic reduction was not optional

Another major theme in the project was that storing or testing large labelled samples without deduplication is wasteful. Once Python had generated random candidates, the next question was not simply “which ones are witnesses?” but also “how many distinct structures are actually represented here?”

This is exactly where nauty is powerful. It offers canonical-labelling and isomorphism tools that can reduce repeated work. But again, there is a practical gap between theoretical usefulness and operational ease. Once one has a large raw file of candidates, the workflow for deduplicating and rechecking still has to be designed carefully.

11. Progress visibility and usability mattered

A small but real difficulty was that long-running nauty commands can feel opaque while they are running. Even when a command is technically working, it is hard to know how far along it is or whether the current pipeline is sensible. This matters a lot in exploratory work, because one often wants feedback before committing hours to a poor setup.

That is one reason why timing wrappers and structured output became useful:

Timing a generation-and-filter pipeline
time ./gengL -q -D10 43 10 | ./pickg -q -D6:10 > ~/Documents/graphs_43v_10e_maxdeg_6_to_10.g6

This does not solve the problem, but it gives at least some information about whether the chosen approach is remotely practical.

12. Why this was harder than it first appeared

Looking back, the real computational difficulty was not just “Ramsey numbers are hard.” More specifically, the hard part was trying to bridge four distinct layers at once:

  • the mathematical layer, where one wants plausible structural criteria;
  • the graph-generation layer, where nauty can impose some but not all constraints efficiently;
  • the data-handling layer, where graph6 files and pipelines must be managed correctly;
  • and the testing layer, where witness checks must be both logically correct and computationally cheap.

Each of those layers is manageable in isolation. The difficulty comes from having to make them work together on genuinely large inputs.

13. A beginner-friendly way to start with nauty for problems like this

For someone approaching nauty with Ramsey-style questions in mind, a good workflow is:

  • start with very small graphs where outputs can be checked by hand,
  • learn the behaviour of geng and pickg on tiny examples,
  • save outputs in graph6,
  • write a small Python parser and verify it on known cases,
  • only then move to larger edge ranges and degree windows,
  • and treat every surprising large-scale result as something to be audited, not trusted immediately.

That gradual approach would have saved confusion in several places. On problems of this scale, a bug discovered late can invalidate an enormous amount of work.

15. What this project taught me

The main lesson was that computation near R(5,5) is not just a matter of more CPU time. The real issue is that one needs a workflow that is mathematically selective, computationally realistic, and carefully debugged at every stage.

nauty is extremely powerful, but power alone is not enough. Around this problem, one quickly encounters the limits of direct generation, the limits of partial filtering, the limits of random sampling, and the limits of trusting code that has not been validated on small cases.

That is why this project became, for me, not only a study of Ramsey candidates but also a study of how combinatorial computation breaks down, where it can be repaired, and what kinds of reductions are still needed before a laptop-level search becomes genuinely persuasive.
