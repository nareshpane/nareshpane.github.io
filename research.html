Research

This page gathers some of my current computational and mathematical research interests. The projects below reflect my interest in graph theory, experimentation, and careful quantitative analysis.

  • Testing the Limits of the nauty Package for Checking Ramsey Number (5,5)
    Computational experiments on graph generation, pruning strategies, non-isomorphic reduction, and feasibility questions arising in the search around the lower bound for \(R(5,5)\).
  • Empirically Validating Evolution of Giant Components in Random Graphs
    A computational study of the emergence and growth of giant components in random graphs, with attention to simulation, repeated sampling, and comparison with theoretical limiting behavior.
  • Preliminary Tests into the Reed–Solomon Proximity Gap Conjecture
    An exploratory study intended to give a concrete flavor of why the Reed–Solomon proximity gap question remains unresolved, and why it is difficult to test computationally once one approaches the Johnson-radius regime and beyond.
  • Animations for Some Basic Graphs
    Visualization of graphs is key to understanding their behavior. I vibecoded in Python to notice some basic graphs, including animations where possible.
  • Devnagari OCR Using Tesseract in Linux
    Optical Character Recognition (OCR) is widely deployed tool. This is an attempt to understand how these tools perform in Devnagari scripts, using Nepali language source as a test. It is an attempt to also understand if a singular PDF file can be converted into a single editable word document.
  • Plantri in Linux for Exploring Four-color Theorem
    Four-colour theorem is a simple to understand but difficult to prove theorem that basically says that any map with any kind and number of borders can be colored by four colors such that no partion of the map is adjacent to another partion having the same color. Here, I attempt to use Plantri, a tool that generates planar graphs. A map with various partitions is effectively a planer graph, with each partition a node and each adjacency an edge.
  • Computational Burden of Graph Generation as Number of Nodes Increase
    Even if we keep a single copy of an isomorphic class, the number of graphs (all non-isomorphic) increases rapidly that even supercomputers may become a joke at some point. This is an attempt at such demonstration.
  • Computationally Replicating Four Theorems from Erdos-Renyi 1959 Paper on Random Graphs
    I have long strugged to understand the implications of the four theorems derived in Erdos-Renyi (1959) paper on Random graphs. Along with a 1956 paper by Gilbert, these two papers are strinkingly close and are said to have started research on random graph. In this page, I try to computatinally replicate four theorems from 1959 paper.
